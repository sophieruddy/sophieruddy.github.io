Making Playtime Accessible

January 2025 - Present


Team: Christina Feng, Christian Oscar-Ramierez, Hayden Williams
Role: Project Lead

Problem Statement Methodology Design Requirements Solutions What's Next
Project Overview

Children with physical and cognitive disabilities at The Park School lacked a reliable play device that could withstand daily use and securely fit varying wheelchair tables. As Project Lead, I guided a team of four through stakeholder interviews, observations, and iterative ideation to deliver two solutions — the Social PlaySpace and the Pegboard — both of which are actively used at the school today. The process revealed a key insight: what users articulate as a need may not reflect the experience they truly hope to resolve. By looking past the stated constraint of wheelchair compatibility, we arrived at a solution the client didn't know was possible.

Problem Statement

Children aged 3-22 at The Park School with physical and cognitive disabilities need a reliable customizable device that increases accessibility in leisurely play.


Current solutions cannot securely attach to variable wheelchair tables, place limitations on child engagement, break down during transport, and fall apart during play.

Methodology

Initial Steps

After receiving a preliminary problem statement, my group dove into secondary research to learn about student abilities, exisiting solutions, wheelchair varieties, and available toys. To foster a productive team environment and establish myself as a proactive and passionate project lead, I came into the meeting with a clear agenda, flexible GANTT and RAM charts, and a running minutes document. I wanted everyone to feel confident in their role, and in me as a reliable leader, after our first team meeting.

A flowchart of our design process

Observing Students and Interviewing Teachers

My team first met with stakeholders to understand the functionalities and pain points of the current solutions. After these interviews, and observations of the children using the devices, my team created a list of initial design requirements.


Our mockups exposed assumptions my team had made in our intial needs statements. So, we needed to revise our design requirements to encompass more solutions!


Ideating to Address Security, Customization, and Engagement

I led my team through multiple ideation sessions using strategies such as proposing "How might we" statements, addressing one requirement at a time, and encouraging outrageous solutions. From these sessions, we narrowed down our collection to five ideas and presented the mock ups to our clients with their respective added functionalities and potential iterations. We prepared specific questions for each mockup to explore anticipated issues and potential use cases in addition to a general set of questions to gauge likes/desires of the design.



Mockup Takeaways

Below, are the six mockups we presented to our clients at The Park School and the most influential learning that drove future iterations.

Grooves Tubes Pegboard Strap Baking Sheet Umbrella

Trading badge reels for nylon string increases object permanence. Reproducibility is not a priority.

Interchangeable device is impractical; only toys should be exchanged

Security on multiple surfaces is desired.

The solution must not be directly attached to the user’s body.

Negative space adds value for customization.

Taking away wheelchair variability and adding social component was well received.

AHA!

Although the client originally asked for a device suited for varying wheelchair tables, my team resolved the issue of device security by removing this variable entirely. We saw through initial constraints to address the clients true need and presented a desirable solution the client didn’t know was possible and therefore couldn’t articulate.

Through mockup feedback, we were able to learn more about our client’s desires and priorities. My team refined our design requirements based on what we uncovered before moving towards our final solutions.

Design Requirements

  • Stable during heavy usage
  • Customizable for individual student needs
  • Easily transported
  • Easy to clean
  • Safe for children

What's Next

The Park School actively uses both of our delivered solutions to engage students in leisurely play. I know because, as of January 2026, I am the active project lead for the Social PlaySpace project again. My new team and I are iterating on the previous prototype to:

  • improve customization mechanisms
  • increase toy visibility
  • increase wheelchair accessibility
  • increase ease of transport

This project taught me that what users can articulate as product needs may not best address the experience they are hoping to resolve. I hope to take this sentiment into future work by thinking about the end goal a user desires before narrowing in on a design embodiment.
